What is an LLM?

LLM stands for Large Language Model. An LLM is an artificial intelligence model trained to understand, generate, summarize, translate, and reason about human language.

LLMs are trained on very large datasets that may include books, websites, code, articles, conversations, and other text-based resources. During training, the model learns patterns in language and becomes good at predicting the next token in a sequence.

For example, if the input is "The sky is", the model may predict that the next likely word or token is "blue". By repeating this process many times, LLMs can generate complete answers, explanations, code, and structured content.

Real-world examples of LLMs include GPT-5.5 by OpenAI, Claude Sonnet 4.6 by Anthropic, and Gemini 3.1 Pro by Google.

What is a Token?

A token is a small chunk of text that an LLM uses to read and generate language. A token can be a full word, part of a word, punctuation mark, number, or symbol. On average, one token is roughly 3-4 characters or about three quarters of an English word.

Tokens matter because LLM providers use them to measure context windows and pricing. The context window shows how much text the model can process in one request. Pricing is also usually calculated by the number of input and output tokens.

For example, the sentence "Hello, world!" might be split into tokens like "Hello", ",", " world", and "!". The exact split depends on the tokenizer used by the model.

English and Azerbaijani can consume tokens differently. English words are common in many training datasets and are often tokenized efficiently. Azerbaijani words may use suffixes and forms that can sometimes be split into more tokens. For example, an Azerbaijani word like "evlərimizdən" may be divided into several smaller pieces, while a common English word may use fewer tokens.

This means that the same idea written in Azerbaijani may sometimes use more tokens than the English version, depending on the exact words and the model tokenizer.
