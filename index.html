What is an LLM?

LLM stands for Large Language Model. It is an artificial intelligence model trained to understand, generate, summarize, translate, and reason about human language.

LLMs are trained on very large datasets such as books, websites, code, articles, documentation, and conversations. During training, the model learns language patterns and becomes good at predicting the next token in a sequence.

For example, if the input is "The sky is", the model may predict that the next likely word is "blue". Real-world examples of LLMs include GPT-5.5 by OpenAI, Claude Sonnet 4.6 by Anthropic, and Gemini 3.1 Pro by Google.

What is a Token?

A token is a small chunk of text that an LLM uses to read and generate language. A token can be a full word, part of a word, punctuation mark, number, or symbol. On average, one token is roughly 3-4 characters or about three quarters of an English word.

Tokens matter because LLM providers use them to measure context windows and pricing. The context window shows how much text the model can process in one request. Pricing is also usually calculated based on input and output tokens.

For example, the sentence "Hello, world!" might be split into tokens like "Hello", ",", " world", and "!". The exact split depends on the tokenizer used by the model.

English and Azerbaijani can consume tokens differently. English words are often tokenized efficiently, while Azerbaijani words may include suffixes and forms that are split into more tokens. For example, a word like "evlərimizdən" may be divided into several smaller pieces.
